## DRAFT Overview

Assignment 2 invites you to compare the classification of the Stylo package in R and of a TF-IDF (term frequency inverse document frequency) approach with a corpus of science fiction texts from Project Gutenberg. This assignment builds on concepts and tools we've discussed in class—particularly exploratory data analysis (EDA) using the two methods. It asks you to compare the two approaches and to synthesize your findings in a web-facing written essay with supporting visualizations.

- **Format:** Individual or pairs (maximum 2 people) 
- **Length:** Approximately 1500-2000 words (about an 8- to 10-minute read), plus visuals

## Five Core Elements

This assignment has five core components:

1. **Corpus Research:** You will be provided with a corpus and will be asked to work only with those texts. No corpus choice is required. That corpus will be in a folder in our class drive marked "Assignment 2 corpus." Nonetheless, you will need to do some research into the authors and the texts included. As in Assignment 1, research into the texts and contexts will be essential for your discussion. We took some collective notes about the authors in class. They can be found in Drive. 
2. **Exploratory Analysis:** You will do exploratory research using the two methods comparing the way that the two methods treat the same texts. You will be asked to use a notebook in posit.cloud in order to carry out the research with Stylo. The results of the TF-IDF will be pre-computed for you across a variety of parameters.
3. **Written Synthesis:** Assemble your evidence, analysis, and visuals in a web-published essay in the form of a post that tells a coherent story about your findings. Make sure that your visuals contribute to the telling of your story.  
4. **Integration of Course Materials:**

5. **Decontextualized Prompting:**
As in Response 1, you will be asked to choose 2-3 visuals from your exploratory research and to prompt the LLM of your choice to explain the visuals without any prior context. Experiment with different ways of labeling the graphs and with providing small amounts of context to see what kind of a response you get. Critically evaluate that reponse.

## Guiding Questions

As you write, consider (but don't feel obligated to answer) all of these questions:

- **Background & Expectations:** How can you compare the different methods Stylo for hierarchical cluster and TF-IDF for  for what they purport to analyze? What do you think about the idea of function word analysis or most distinctive word analysis as a way of doing distant reading? 

- **Computational Insights:** How does the wordlist generated by Stylo compare to the loadings that you can visualize in the TF- IDF PCA visualization? What words occur in both of them? What words are specific to one approach? Was the gender of the writers linked to any kind of clustering? 

- **Comparative Insights:** How do the clusters that you get in hierarchical analysis compare to the proximity of the points you get in the TF-IDF PCA visualization? Typically one would say that Stylo is good for detecting authorship and similarity in style of writing and TF-IDF is good for detecting content similarity? Did you find this to be true? Are style and content truly separable?   

- **Trends & Surprises:** Were there any surprises that you found when working with both methods?
